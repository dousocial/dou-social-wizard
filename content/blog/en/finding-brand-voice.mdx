The most common answer to "what should our brand voice be?" is: **"professional but friendly"**. This isn't wrong — it just *says nothing*. Most brands describe themselves the same way.

A working brand voice takes a clear position on **four independent dimensions**. The framework comes from Nielsen Norman Group's classic tone-of-voice work, and we use it in every brand discovery as an agency.

## Four dimensions

### 1. Funny ⇄ Serious

Does the brand have natural humor, or does the gravity of the topic take precedence?

- **Very funny:** Duolingo Twitter, Wendy's
- **Very serious:** McKinsey, an oncology drug company
- **Middle:** Apple — serious but occasionally warm

### 2. Formal ⇄ Casual

Is the brand's dialogue a notarized letter or a coffee chat?

- **Very formal:** Law firms, traditional finance
- **Very casual:** Most D2C brands, lifestyle brands
- **Middle:** Banking apps (formal but UX-friendly)

### 3. Respectful ⇄ Irreverent

Does the brand have an opinion? Does it push boundaries?

- **Very respectful:** Enterprise IT
- **Very irreverent:** Liquid Death, early Dollar Shave Club
- **Middle:** Patagonia (irreverent but respectful)

### 4. Enthusiastic ⇄ Matter-of-fact

Is the energy high, or calm and considered?

- **Very enthusiastic:** Sports brands (Red Bull, Nike)
- **Very matter-of-fact:** Mediterranean design brands, luxury hotels
- **Middle:** Apple — enthusiastic launches + calm product detail

## Practical exercise

Score your brand on each dimension from 1-5:

- Funny ⇄ Serious: ___ (1=very funny, 5=very serious)
- Formal ⇄ Casual: ___
- Respectful ⇄ Irreverent: ___
- Enthusiastic ⇄ Matter-of-fact: ___

Then ask: **"If I asked 5 people on my team, would they all write the same numbers?"**

If the answer is "no", you don't have a brand voice — you have personal preferences.

## From scores to examples

Defining voice with numbers isn't enough — you need to **clarify with examples**. For each dimension, write 2-3 sentences:

> "Our voice sounds like:"
> "Our voice does **not** sound like:"

Example (mid-serious, casual, mid-irreverent, matter-of-fact):

✅ "We didn't add this feature because our users didn't ask for it."
✅ "Most software companies don't tell you what they can't do. We do."

❌ "Aligned with valuable customer feedback, we deliver innovative solutions." (too generic, too corporate)
❌ "OMG this feature is gonna be so cool 🔥🔥🔥" (too enthusiastic, breaks brand boundary)

## Spreading voice across the team

Write a brand guide that contains the voice. Page count doesn't matter — **example quality** does. Your team should be able to consult this when writing a new tweet, email or post.

Practically:

- Page 1: 4-dimension brand score + one-line rationale
- Page 2: ✅/❌ examples (at least 10 pairs)
- Page 3: A "do not" list (banned words, clichés)
